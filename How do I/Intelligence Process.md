# Intelligence Process: Information

## Information in the Intelligence Process

An intelligence analyst collects information from large amounts of data and different sources. The more diverse the sources and types of information, the higher the chance of accurate analysis—but also the harder it is. This is because comparing and verifying information from multiple sources reduces the risk of error, yet handling the volume becomes challenging, potentially causing "information overload."

The most effective way to avoid this is a selective approach and proper classification of information. Analysts must first study the nature of information. This section focuses on the first stage of the analyst-information relationship.

### VII.1. The Path to Strategic Information

Strategic information provides decision-makers with general strategic assessments and insights into potential or ongoing conflicts, including cultural, technological, and geographical dimensions. To produce strategic information, two key steps must be addressed: **A) Approach to information** and **B) Categorization of information**. Without knowing how and what we know, strategic information cannot be generated.

#### a) Approaches to Information

There are five basic approaches:

1. **Belief-based:** Accepting information as true without question. Example: superstitions.
2. **Authority-based:** Trusting what authoritative figures say. Useful as a reference but limited for research.
3. **Rationalist:** Uses reasoning and deduction to infer unseen relationships (A → B → C implies A → C).
4. **In-depth observation:** Drawing conclusions from careful personal observation; not systematic, useful for building structured research.
5. **Scientific method:** Systematic collection, classification, and analysis of data; self-correcting and continuously updated.

Analysts should avoid the first two approaches. Approaches 3 and 4 can assist the fifth, scientific approach.

#### b) Categorization of Information: Data, News, Knowledge

Strategic analysts must categorize "information" not only by perspective but also by type. Four main categories exist:

- **Data:** Independent, raw facts. Example: number of cars in a parking lot.
- **News:** Aggregated data providing context; alone, it is not intelligence.
- **Knowledge:** Analyzed data with meaning. Example: combining unemployment rise and car parking decrease to infer industrial weakness.
- **Intelligence:** Collected, classified, verified information formulated to support decision-making. Four components: cost, source, method, target.

Intelligence can also be defined using Robert Steele's framework:

- **Data** – raw text, signals, or images.
- **News** – aggregated verified data.
- **Analysis/Intelligence** – processed information supporting specific decisions.

Analysts work with **hard data** (facts, statistics) and **soft data** (opinions, rumors). Both are necessary for comprehensive analysis.

## VII.2. Fundamental Information

Fundamental information is static or slowly changing data, such as:

- Geographic location of a country  
- Population structure  
- Social conditions  
- Political structure  
- Education  
- Moral and religious values  

These provide a baseline for strategic analysis. Dynamic information updates and complements these fundamentals.

## VII.3. Dynamic Information

Dynamic information captures potential powers, political goals, and future possibilities. Unlike fundamental information, it requires analysis and imagination to interpret.

## VII.4. Open Source Intelligence (OSINT)

Much fundamental information comes from **open sources**, including newspapers, books, internet, and interviews. Open source intelligence (OSINT) is crucial and can be surprisingly valuable—even for "highly secret" insights. Advantages:

- Cost-effective  
- Abundant sources  
- Easy to collect

Limitations include misleading information, especially in politically sensitive areas. Analysts must critically evaluate open source data. Historical examples demonstrate OSINT’s effectiveness, such as Robert Steele producing a complete intelligence report using only open sources faster than the CIA.

Open sources also include controlled and uncontrolled sources:

- **Controlled sources:** Assigned or question-based sources  
- **Uncontrolled sources:** Public media, websites, news agencies
