# Intelligence Analysis: Key Concepts for SOC/CTI

## Analysis Missions
In cybersecurity intelligence, the analysis phase is crucial. Analysts focus on interpreting collected data to inform decisions. Two primary approaches are:

- **Data-driven analysis:** Conclusions rely on raw data accuracy and completeness. Example: Determining if a malware campaign is active based on logs and network indicators.  
- **Concept-driven analysis:** Analysts work with incomplete or ambiguous data, forming hypotheses and testing them against available evidence. Example: Predicting attacker TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures) based on threat actor behavior patterns.

## Analysis Methods
Analysts use cognitive and creative methods to understand adversary behavior:

- **Backward reasoning:** Starting from observed incidents to reconstruct potential attack paths.  
- **Role-playing:** Imagining the attacker’s perspective to anticipate next moves.  
- **Devil’s advocacy:** Challenging assumptions to test alternate scenarios.  
- **Multiple hypothesis evaluation:** Comparing several possible explanations to identify the most likely scenario.

## Analysis Techniques
Key techniques for SOC/CTI include:

- **Statistical models:** Quantitative analysis of network or endpoint data to detect anomalies.  
- **Comparative hypothesis analysis:** Testing multiple attack scenarios against indicators.  
- **Attack and victim profiling:** Understanding adversary behavior and target characteristics.  
- **Cause-effect analysis:** Mapping attack chains and potential impacts.  
- **SWOT and T-matrix frameworks:** Evaluating strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats in incidents.  
- **Link analysis:** Visualizing relationships between indicators, actors, and campaigns.

## Practical Benefits
Applying these methods and techniques helps analysts:

- Prioritize threats effectively.  
- Predict adversary moves.  
- Support faster, more informed response decisions.
